A *centrosymmetric* or *compensated* crystal looks spin-degenerate from a
global symmetry standpoint — yet site-projected (sub-lattice-local)
wavefunctions still carry a momentum-resolved spin polarisation that
controls experimentally accessible response functions.

I established the framework first for centrosymmetric non-magnetic crystals
([Nat. Commun. 2019](/publications/natcomm-2019-hidden-rashba/) ) and then extended it to collinear antiferromagnets
([Nat. Commun. 2023](/publications/natcomm-2023-hidden-spin/)). The picture
has since been generalised to layered Ruddlesden–Popper oxides ([Nano Letters
2026](/publications/nanolet-2026-rp-oxides/)).

**What it enables.** Hidden spin polarisation expands the candidate pool
for spintronics from a small set of low-symmetry magnets to a large
fraction of the *centrosymmetric* periodic table — directly useful for
high-throughput screening pipelines and ML/AI materials discovery.
