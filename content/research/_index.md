AI compute and energy demand have tripled global data-centre load since
2014, while Moore's-law scaling has reached its physical limit. New
microelectronic materials — and a deeper symmetry-based understanding of
them — are needed to support the next generation of computing architectures.

My research uses **first-principles calculations, symmetry analysis, and
crystal-physics/chemistry models** to simulate solid-state electronic materials at the
atomic scale, with the goal of understanding their underlying physical
effects and *designing* new microelectronic materials. The work runs along
various materials classes:

**Semiconductors** — The maturity of CMOS technology ensures continued reliance on silicon platforms. To maximize the potential of electronic materials – such as ferroelectric materials  – they need to be integrated with semiconductors. Yet, this integration faces challenges -- lattice mismatch, bonding incompatibilities, interface defects, and unstable ferroic states in thin films. Addressing these challenges requires atomic-level understanding of materials and a reliable and innovative way to do high-fidelity simulations of materials and devices[ Read more](/research/direct-bandgap-semiconductors/)

**Novel magnetic materials** — Antiferromagnets (AFMs) offer zero stray fields and terahertz spin dynamics, making them ideal for ultrafast, energy-efficient spintronics. Yet, their spin-degenerate bands have limited practical use. Recent works have uncovered a new class of AFMs — certain collinear and noncollinear AFMs — that exhibit momentum-dependent net spin splitting from a specific combination of magnetic order and crystal symmetry. The strict symmetry criteria for this class narrow the materials pool and exclude many promising candidates [Read more](/research/magnetism/)

**Ferroic materials** — Ferroic materials, with intrinsic bistability and fast switching dynamics, have been pursued for decades as a pathway toward nonvolatile logic and memory, significantly reducing the energy cost while transferring data back and forth between memory and processor. Nonetheless, how does the structure instability, covalency and correlation cooperate/compete to stabilize a ferroic phase remain a rich source for advancing fundamental materials theory. Despite decades of research, materials combine multiple ferroic orders, such as ferroelectricity and magnetism, remain rare, and their integration into practical devices is still limited [Read more](/research/ferroic-materials/)
